Introduction to Oil & Gas

Petroleum Chemistry & Composition

- Crude oils are complex mixtures containing many different hydrocarbon compounds that vary in appearance and composition from one oil field to another.

- Crude oils range in consistency from water to tar-like solids, and in color from clear to black. An "average“ crude oil contains about 84% carbon, 14% hydrogen, 1%-3% sulfur, and less than 1% each of nitrogen, oxygen, metals, and salts.

- Crude oils are generally classified as paraffinic, naphthenic, or aromatic, based on the predominant proportion of similar hydrocarbon molecules.

- Mixed-base crudes have varying amounts of each type of hydrocarbon.

- Crude oils that contain appreciable quantities of hydrogen sulfide or other reactive sulfur compounds are called "sour.“ Those with less sulfur are called "sweet."

Three Principal Groups or Series of Hydrocarbon Compounds that Occur Naturally:

Paraffins:

The paraffinic series of hydrocarbon compounds found in crude oil have the general formula $C_nH_{2n+2}$ and can be either straight chains (normal) or branch chains (isomers) of carbon atoms.

The lighter, straight-chain paraffin molecules are found in gases and paraffin waxes. Examples of straight-chain molecules are methane, ethane, propane, and butane.

Petroleum Chemistry & Composition

Aromatics:

These are unsaturated ring-type (cyclic) compounds which react readily because they have carbon atoms that are deficient in hydrogen. All aromatics have at least one benzene ring (a single-ring compound characterized by three double bonds alternating with three single bonds between six carbon atoms) as part of their molecular structure.

Naphthenes:

Naphthenes are saturated hydrocarbon groupings with the general formula CnH2n, arranged in the form of closed rings (cyclic) and found in all fractions of crude oil except the very lightest. Single-ring naphthenes (monocycloparaffins) with five and six carbon atoms are more predominate, with two-ring naphthenes (dicycloparaffins) are found in the heavier ends of naphtha.

Formation of Oil & Gas

- Most crude oil and natural gas originate from plant and animal life that thrived millions of years ago in swamps and oceans. These organic materials were deposited with mud and silt from streams and rivers.

- The sediments eventually hardened to form sedimentary rock. Heat and pressure transformed the soft parts of the plants and animals into solid, liquid or gaseous hydrocarbons known as fossil fuels - coal, crude oil or natural gas.

- The oil and gas bearing structure is typically a porous rock such as sandstone or washed out limestone. The sand might have been laid down as desert dunes or sea floor.

- Oil and gas deposits form as organic material (tiny plants and animals) deposited in earlier geological periods, typically 100 to 200 million years ago, under, over or with the sand or silt, is transformed by high temperature and pressure into hydrocarbons.

Dissolved gas drive.

- Oil has gas dissolved in it. As the gas escapes from the oil, the bubbles expand and this expansion produces a force on the oil which drives it through the reservoir toward the well and assists in lifting it to the surface.

- It is generally considered the least effective type of drive yielding only 15% to 25% of the oil originally contained in the reservoir.

Gas Cap drive.

- Some reservoirs contain more gas than can be dissolved in the oil at the reservoir pressure and temperature. The surplus gas, since it is lighter than oil, rises to the top of the reservoir and forms a gas cap over the oil. The gas expands to drive the oil toward the wellbore.

- The Gas Cap Drive is more effective then Dissolved Gas Drive alone yielding from 25% to 50% of the oil contained in the reservoir.

Water drive.

- Vast quantities of water provide a great store of energy which can aid the production of oil and gas.

- The energy supplied by the salt water comes from the expansion of water as pressure in the petroleum reservoir is reduced by production of oil and gas.

- Water is generally considered incompressible, but will actually compress and expand about one part in 2500 per 100 psi change in pressure. When the enormous quantities of water present are considered, this expansion results in a significant amount of energy which can aid the drive of petroleum to the surface. The water also moves and displaces oil and gas in an upward direction out of the lower parts of the reservoir.

- The "Water Drive" is the most efficient of the primary drive mechanisms, capable of yielding up to 50% of the original oil in place.

Wellheads & Gathering

- Well fluid is received at the surface through Wellhead for each well.

- Wellhead piping is comprising of :
--> Wing Valve, SSVs (can be part of HIPPS), Choke Valve and 100 mt. piping up to the last isolation valve at Tie-in to the Flowline.

- Wellhead piping (ANSI rating) and its components (API rating) shall be designed for :
* WHCIP at surface for natural flowing well.
* ESP Shut off pressure at surface for ESP lifted well.
* WHCIP or Gas lift source pressure whichever is higher for Gas Lifted wells.

- ESD Trips @ Wellhead
1. High High Oil Pressure Trip to close SSVs (can be part of HIPPS) and SSSV and trip CI Injection. (Also close Gas side SSV and SDV in case of a Gas lifted well)

2. Low low Oil Pressure Trip to close SSVs and trip CI Injection. (Also close Gas side SSV and SDV in case of a Gas lifted well)

3. Confirmed HC gas detection (2ooN) closes SSSV, SSVs and trip CI Injection. (Also close Gas side SSV and SDV in case of a Gas lifted well)

4. Confirmed Toxic gas detection (2ooN) closes SSSV, SSVs and trip CI Injection. (Also close Gas side SSV and SDV in case of a Gas lifted well)

5. Confirmed Fire (IR Flame detectors) and High / Low Fusible Plugs closes SSSV, SSVs and trip CI Injection. (Also close Gas side SSV and SDV in case of a Gas lifted well).

- Conventionally as per BAU,
1. Flowlines from several wells gathered through Gathering manifolds in adjacent RDS.

2. One or more production headers and test headers are accommodated (By project phasing) in RDS either to collect productions or for testing purposes.

3. Collected well fluid from RDS is sent to CDS by one or more transfer lines. This transfer lines are made piggable.

4. A multi phase Hydraulic study is carried out in PIPESIM for determining sizing of gathering network i.e. flow lines and transfer lines and back pressure at RDS and at each wellhead for further engineering.

INTRODUCTION

Specific unit operation used for separating vapor, oil and water phases of a produced wellhead fluid are called separation.

- Recall …why Multi stage flash-separation is required as first in sequence of operations ?
1. To help in achieving Oil spec such as BS & W
2. To enhance recovery of oil
3. To ensure oil spec in produced water for further treatment, injection or disposal

** Main reason ……”If gas and water are removed from oil at very first step of sequence , significant equipment size hence cost reduction is possible while further processing of oil”

PRINCIPLES OF SEPARATION

Momentum
- Fluid phases with different densities will have different momentum. If a two phase stream changes direction sharply, greater momentum will not allow the particles of heavier phase to turn as rapidly as lighter fluid, so separation occurs.

Gravity settling
- Liquid droplets will settle out of a gas phase if the gravitational force acting on the droplet is greater than the drag force of the

Coalescing
- Very small droplets which can not be separated practically by gravity, can be coalesced to form larger droplets. Coalescing devices such as demister screens, vane elements in separators force gas to follow a tortuous path. The momentum of the droplets causes them to collide with other droplets or coalescing device, forming larger droplets.

Liquid collection
- This section provides the required residence time to liquid phase. In case of two immiscible liquid phases, this liquid collection section gives settling time required for separation of two phases.

Stabilization / Sweetening

Introduction

- Process of removing dissolved natural gases such as C1, C2 and C3 as completely as possible while leaving the maximum amounts of C4 (and C5 and heavier) in the crude oil to meet RVP spec of sales crude oil is termed as Stabilization of Crude Oil.

- Crude oil can be stabilized by passing it through a series of flash drums or Vapor-liquid separator vessels at successively low pressures followed by a tray towers with or without re-boiler.

- Stabilization can also sweeten the crude because H2S has a boiling point intermediate to that of C2 and C3.

- Addition of stripping gas enhances the sweetening process.

Why Stabilization / Sweetening of Crude required ?

- Dissolved lighter hydrocarbon gas in crude results in two major safety issues:
--> Higher vapor pressure when pipeline, storage vessel or tanker exposed to direct sun light or higher ambient temperature… This may lead to damage in worst case.
--> Pumping cavitation during transfer operation

- If H2S content in crude is excessive ( > 50 ppm), that leads to potential health hazard and corrosion issue at downstream processes.

Why Reid Vapor Pressure is set 10 psia (winter) & 6.2 psia (summer) ?

- Setting RVP < 10 psia ensures that True Vapor Phase (more properly Bubble Point Pressure) of crude is limited 13 – 15 psia.

- i.e. crude with RVP <10 psia can even be stored at atmospheric pressure without having any major vaporizing or pressurization issue.

- Also on other hand, economic goal of stabilization i.e. to maximize stock tank oil recovery (0API gravity) is met by leaving the maximum amounts of C4 and (C5 and heavier) in the crude and removing C1, C2 & C3 as completely as possible.

- Setting RVP < 10 psia also ensure meeting H2S spec with or without stripping gas.

Design Check Required

- Operating point for rated flow is to the left of best efficiency point (BEP) and within 10% from BEP.
- Locate operating point for turndown flow.
- Viscosity correction applied by vendor if > 5 cSt.
- Motor power is sufficient for end-of-curve operation.
- Pump shut-off pressure is lower than design pressure of downstream equipment.
- Is there sufficient margin between pump impeller size and max impeller size?
- Pump is self-priming.

Pumps Operation Minimum Flow Considerations

- When liquid is pumped, its temperature increases as energy resulting from inefficiency is converted to heat
- In normal operation, temperature rise is negligible
- In shut off condition, all the energy gets converted to heat. Since there is no flow through the pump, the liquid in the pumps rapidly heats and eventually vaporizes thereby damaging the pump.
- It is , therefore, important to ensure some minimum flow through the pump at all times.
- This is achieved by providing Minimum Flow bypass control from pump discharge to suction side.
- Usually, value of minimum flow is specified by vendor. However, for initial design, it is usual to consider 30% of rated flow.

Flare & Blow down Awareness

- All the equipment and process systems shall be protected from Over pressurization.
--> Initial layer of Over pressure protection shall be any IPF i.e. Instrumented Protected Functions such as ESD trip on high high pressure or reducing pressure by flaring PCV.
--> Ultimate layer of protection shall be either inherently safe i.e. Fully rated or HIPPS protection with HIPPS leakage PSV.
--> Or. Pressure Safety Valve normally set at the design pressure of the system.

- Operating pressure of process systems shall be reduced to safe level to avoid rupture or failure due to thermal stress in case of a fire breakout.
--> Blow down orifice is designed to reduce the system maximum operating pressure in 15 minutes to 50% of design pressure or 6.9 bar g whichever is lower. Transfer Data.
